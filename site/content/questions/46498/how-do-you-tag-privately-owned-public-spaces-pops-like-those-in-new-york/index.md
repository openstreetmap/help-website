<p>In the US, for spaces like the one described by the wikipedia link in the question, it's unlikely the permission to use the space can be revoked by the owner (it would be done by some public planning commission).</p>
<p>In general I think the meaning of access=permissive is being bent quite a ways when it is used in the US at all (lots of private access is treated as public, but it's often the case than an individual can be asked to leave for arbitrary reasons).</p>

<p>For privately-owned public spaces in the UK, "permissive" (or even "private") would be the more likely - I'd expect that the owner <em>does</em> have the right to revoke access. As an aside, see this recent article discussing the issues with one particular project:</p>
<p><a href="http://www.theguardian.com/uk-news/2015/nov/06/garden-bridge-mobile-phone-signals-tracking-london">http://www.theguardian.com/uk-news/2015/nov/06/garden-bridge-mobile-phone-signals-tracking-london</a></p>
<p>Obviously the Guardian has a far from neutral POV here, but the links from there such as <a href="http://planning-docs.lambeth.gov.uk/AnitePublicDocs/00590159.pdf">http://planning-docs.lambeth.gov.uk/AnitePublicDocs/00590159.pdf</a> are valid.</p>
<p>One more thing - rather than "access" consider those modes that can actually use the space (foot, possibly bicycle, possibly others). Just "access=permissive" implies all modes have the same access, which is unlikely.</p>
