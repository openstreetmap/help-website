<div class="question-body">
<p>The book <a href="http://www.openstreetmap.info/content/index.html"><em>OpenStreetMap: Using and Enhancing the Free Map of the World</em></a> by Ramm, Topf, and Chilton (<em>urn:isbn:978-1-906860-11-0</em>) suggests using relations with <code>type=dual_carriageway</code> to help express that parallel ways form part of a divided dual carriageway. <strong>Are there any consumers of this data</strong>, either for rendering or routing, anywhere in the world? (And, relatedly, does it matter? If we build it, might they not come?)</p>
<p>There are <abbr title="2011-06-05T14:09:00+0100">currently</abbr> 997 <a href="http://taginfo.openstreetmap.de/tags/type=dual_carriageway#wiki">uses of this tag</a> for relations in the OSM database, but no machine-accessible documentation, so I might upgrade <a href="http://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Relations/Proposed/Dual_carriageways">the proposal</a> for it to something documented as a de facto tag.</p>

<div class="answer-body">
<p>In mind is that in the UK, roads (excluding motorways) specifically marked as a dual carriageway would be subject to a national speed limit of 70 mph for cars, and 50 mph for HGVs; compared with 60 and 40 on single carriageways. These would be usually signified with a 'dual carriageway' sign at the commencement, and the upside down Y sign at the end (as opposed to the two way traffic sign, which may be used additionally to provide a further warning.</p>
<p>Short separated ways at say, intersections, as far as my understanding of traffic laws goes at least; would not be a type of dual carriageway, and would therefore have the lower limits - therefore the tag should probably be documented properly.</p>
