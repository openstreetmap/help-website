<div class="answer-body">
<p>The approach taken may vary according to the citation policies of specific journals. There is a <a href="https://scholar.google.com/scholar?hl=en&amp;as_sdt=0%2C5&amp;q=openstreetmap&amp;btnG=">huge research literature</a> on OpenStreetMap (probably tens of thousands of papers now), and probably the easiest thing is to check through some of that for examples. One type is a very simple "<code>OpenStreetMap. </code><a href="https://www.openstreetmap.org/"><code>https://www.openstreetmap.org/</code></a><code> (2020).</code>" in <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-020-00739-0#Bib1">Stowell <em>et al</em>. (2020)</a>.</p>
<p>Other types of citation:</p>
<ul>
<li>General overview: 2 books have been published on OSM, Bennett (2010) &amp; Ramm, Topf &amp; Chilton (2011)</li>
<li>Multi-author books: classic example would be Arsanjani, J. J., Zipf, A., Mooney, P. &amp; Helbich, M. <em>OpenStreetMap in GIScience: experiences, research, and applications.</em> Lecture notes in geoinformation and cartography (Springer, 2015).</li>
<li>Early research work on OSM: authors such as Haklay, Zipf &amp; Mooney pioneered academic investigation of OSM, and consequently these early papers are influential</li>
<li>Data. Probably best to cite when accessed and which site (e.g., OSM Planets or Geofabrik downloads.</li>
<li>Wiki. Include access date</li>
<li>Software, e.g., Nominatim. Ideally include date used &amp; version</li>
</ul>
<p>The latter three points will be significant if anyone wishes to replicate reported work.</p>
<p>One last point, if data derived from OSM is a published output this must be licensed appropriately (typically under ODbL).</p>
