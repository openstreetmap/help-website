<p>I am assuming by the words public footpath you are talking England or Wales.</p>
<p>It is unreasonable to expect a walker to climb a gate, or fence, in order to negotiate a public right of way, if however there is a stile then that I think is a legal alternative,</p>
<p>If there is a locked gate with no alternative stile, then that should be reported to the local highway authority (usually county council).</p>
<p>On a bridleway, there should be an openable gate,</p>
<p>Many highway authorities are in the process of replacing stiles with kissing gate, but that obviously takes both time and money.</p>
<p>Your local rights of way section may have information on which paths have been converted to kissing gates.</p>
<p>I would certainly not map a locked gate, with no alternative on a right of way without reporting it.</p>
<p>I do map stiles and kissing gates, it is a great shame they are not rendered.</p>

<p>In the OSM database, we use <a href="http://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Key:access">access tags</a> on <a href="http://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Key:barrier">barriers</a> to express who can pass through the barrier. So gates that can be opened by pedestrians should at least get a foot=yes tag.</p>
<p>As for visualizing this information, Overpass Turbo is your best bet, as described in the <a href="https://help.openstreetmap.org/questions/36588/how-do-i-view-stiles-on-the-map">stile question</a>.</p>
