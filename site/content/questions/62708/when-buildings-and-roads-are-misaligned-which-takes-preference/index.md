So please upload those GPXes.</p>

<p>Both are important, realignment of a background layer as reference has to be done to seriously plot or correct misaligned elements in OSM.</p>
<p>As recommended, the best way to make sure there is no offset on a layer is to overlap several straight GPS traces meeting on a crossway and at least going to the four cardinal/intercardinal directions (Strava used to be a good reference) and align according the average. Beware that even GPS traces have an offset on curves, steep hills, near cliffs and because of the GPS tech precision limits (use Glonass in addition for a better result).</p>
<p>Also very important, beware of tilted satellite photo shots and steep oblique surfaces (steep hills, mountains, etc). The exact position gets deformed depending on the angle between the satellite photos and/or those surfaces. How to check:<br />
- if there are steep hills, check on a topographic map.<br />
- if the photo shots are tilted: search a close area with a building (higher buildings are better), the more you see its walls the more it's tilted, if you only see the roof, that's perfect. Select a less tilted image sat. source or realign the layer accordingly with the previous recommendation. Of course depending of the area the tilting changes on a same satellite imagery because of the satellite/Earth movements.</p>
