<p>Just wanted to check that it's OK to reference this data without tracing, ie I can check on the nature of a building - for example in the Bing aerial data I see what might be a building, on Google I can see it's a temple, it's named and there are photos available.</p>
<ul>
<li>How much of that info can I use?</li>
<li>What's the best way to reference it for other mappers (and possibly for rescuers on the ground)?</li>
</ul>

<p>Just my personal opinion as a mapper, IANAL:</p>
<p>That would be a very very slippery slope (note that you would not only look at a aerial photograph of a building but also using google's georeferencing/positions to find the location). Please just don't use them – maybe except for <em>finding</em> locations to which you want to go <em>on the ground</em> (visit in person) and survey (gps, your personal handwritten/electronic notes, …) and then do your OSM based on your own survey. Also, see the somehow similar (also not about a full tracing) question <a href="/questions/41362/">using-google-satellite-images-to-align-my-paths</a>.</p>
<p>Try other imagery resources OSM is currently allowed to use: currently e.g. Mapbox's aerial images (as preconfigured in iD and JOSM).</p>
<p>As tempting as may be: a HOT activation does not mean to abandon our <a href="https://www.openstreetmap.org/welcome">project's</a> <a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Legal_FAQ#2._Contributing">prin</a><a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/About">ciples</a>. The data which you now add will also stay in the future. If google (or its sat provider) does not want to release images in such situations, it is that way.</p>

<p>General remarks (too long for a comment):</p>
<ul>
<li>OSM is used globally, what is ok in one country may not be permissible in another, given that the goal of OSM is to provide open data that in as unencumbered by 3rd party rights as possible, we should be very conservative.</li>
<li>legal arguing belongs before a court not on the OSM help site and yes there is never a guarantee that you will win so you should realistically consider what it means to loose. Naturally in light of <a href="http://curia.europa.eu/juris/document/document.jsf?docid=161388&amp;doclang=EN">"Database not protected by copyright or the sui generis right — Contractual limitation on the rights of users of the database"</a> google would argue that its imagery is not subject to DB regulation and that their ToS apply.</li>
<li>the proper way to resolve the issue is to ask google for permission, in the past (aka the Haiti earthquake) they have given such permission, you should suggest doing so to HOT</li>
</ul>
