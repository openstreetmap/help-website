<div class="answer-body">
<p>Cartographically burnt and recently felled woodland are often indicated on various maps: notably for woodland management, biodiversity, but also for recreation. There are therefore clearly relevant use-cases for this type of thing. In upland areas where forest regeneration is very slow these areas may persist for decades.</p>
<p>As far as I know any tags in use are entirely experimental and have been used by individual mappers at their own discretion. The following are such examples I could find:</p>
<ul>
<li>keys recently_felled &amp; recently_felled;survey_year, see <a href="https://taginfo.openstreetmap.org/search?q=felled">taginfo</a></li>
<li>key forest=felled (e.g., <a href="https://www.openstreetmap.org/relation/6844565)">https://www.openstreetmap.org/relation/6844565)</a></li>
<li>key burnt, predominantly used in Russia for a variety of landcovers, see <a href="https://taginfo.openstreetmap.org/keys/burnt">taginfo</a></li>
<li>key wood:lost, see <a href="https://taginfo.openstreetmap.org/keys/wood%3Alost.">https://taginfo.openstreetmap.org/keys/wood%3Alost.</a> This allows for wind damage as well as burning</li>
</ul>
<p>Although there is no sign of a single consensus approach to tagging, in many of these cases contributors have recognised how important it is to record the actual date of damage or felling.</p>
<p>I think there are some related points on the Wiki, but I cannot find them at present. Note that in addition to wind, fire, and felling, forests can be damaaged by insect &amp; fungus infestations, avalanches, floods and inundation.</p>
