<p>Imagine a field or a park. It has many locations to reach its boundaries from the surrounding geometry. Within the field itself it is open and people can walk anywhere within it.</p>
<p>How should something like this be mapped? <a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Tag:leisure%3Dpark"><code>leisure=park</code></a> + <a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Key:area"><code>area=yes</code></a>?</p>
<p>An alternative approach considered is to map paths within the field, but all the combinations of possible routes is too much to add.</p>

<div class="answer-body">
<p>Hi, I would suggest leisure=park if it is a recognised park open to the public.</p>
<p>Plus landuse=recreation_ground if the park is used as a recreation ground.</p>
<p>Plus foot=designated to show the status of the whole area as being accessible on foot.</p>
<p>Plus any other relevant access tags, ie dog=leashed, horse=no, cycle=no.</p>
<p>Specific paths, desire path lines or prominent ways can also be mapped to show permanent, through or desire routes.</p>

<td><div class="item-right">
<div class="answer-body">
<p>As far as I know, there are only two tag combos that are generally understood to indicate an omnidirectional foot-navigable area: <strong><code>highway=pedestrian + area=yes</code></strong> and <strong><code>highway=footway + area=yes</code></strong>.</p>
<p>"Park" can mean many things, and if a particular park satisfies the definition of <strong><code>highway=pedestrian</code></strong> or <strong><code>highway=footway</code></strong> then that's a fine approach. But neither of these is appropriate for a grassy area or field.</p>
<p>Note that the <strong><code>area=yes</code></strong> tag is only required for something that's normally a linear way (like a highway). A park mapped as way will already be considered an area, and so adding the <strong><code>area=yes</code></strong> tag to a park will be flagged as an error.</p>
<p>Also, parks often include features that can't be walked directly though, eg, hedges, ponds, playgrounds, or simply challenging terrain. So no sensible routing software would ever consider a park to be an omnidirectional foot-navigable area.</p>
<p>For a public grassy area, consider the tag <strong><code>landuse=recreation_ground</code></strong>, which is sometimes used within parks, and sometimes on its own. IMO it implies an unpaved area that's omnidirectionally navigable by foot -- although I doubt that any current routing software would process it that way.</p>
<p>If you really need to make sure that routers will find a way through the area in question, you need to draw paths or footways. If the area is completely open and there really are no paths, then adding them is a convenient fiction, not ideal but forgivable. This is the best we can do with current tagging, best I understand.</p>
</div>
