<div class="answer-body">
<p>I would only tag the short section of road under the bridge with the maxheight tag. Routers should be able to then avoid the bridge (if they know your vehicle height). Tagging the whole way isn't necessary, and risks any side roads that aren't affected by the height restriction becoming unroutable.</p>
<p>Example road under a railway bridge:</p>
<p><a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/File:Bridge_height_restriction_Worcester.jpg"><img src="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/w/images/a/a6/Bridge_height_restriction_Worcester.jpg" alt="photo" /></a></p>
<p><a href="https://www.openstreetmap.org/way/505620845#map=18/52.15584/-2.15374">The tag goes on a short section of road</a> : <a href="https://www.openstreetmap.org/way/505620845#map=18/52.15584/-2.15374"><img src="/upfiles/maxheight_tagging_example.png" alt="way 505620845 screenshot" /></a></p>

<p><a href="https://help.openstreetmap.org/users/14/tordanik">@Tordanik</a>, in the United States, the maximum-height restriction only applies to the actual point of physical restriction, though you will often see warning signs well ahead of that point.</p>
<p>For something passing over the road, I generally tag the shortest length of road for which passing the obstacle in question is unavoidable. For a tunnel, I put the tag on the way representing the tunnel itself.</p>

<p>At the best, I would tag the highway segment where the legal restriction is applying, let's say "where the road sign showing the restrictions are". Then it's not "you" to decide where you give notice in advance but rather the local authorities taking into account the local environment (and topology). If you give notice too early, e.g. at the intersections for instance, the risk is to exclude valuable routes in routing applications like factories entrances. If you give notice too late, you might take a truck into a narrow road where reverse drive is impossible (but that should never happen since drivers should follow road signs prior to GPS ;-)</p>
