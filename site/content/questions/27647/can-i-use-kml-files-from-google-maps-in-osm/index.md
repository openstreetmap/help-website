<div class="answer-body">
<ol>
<li>In copyright, the default is "you're not allowed to use it unless you have permission from the rights owner". Unless the source has an explicit statement about the license (in which the author might grant you some rights) then the rule is: Ask the author. And "Public, 5 Collaborators" doesn't mean anything.</li>
<li>If there is any reason to suspect that this data set has been created with reference to Google maps or Google aerial imagery - e.g. collaborators highlighting bicycle parking corrals on a Google map - then Google or their suppliers own at least part of the rights, and it is not sufficient to ask those who actually drew the bicycle parking corrals, you'll have to ask Google too (or check their licensing).</li>
</ol>
<p>The likely overall answer to your question is, therefore, no.</p>
