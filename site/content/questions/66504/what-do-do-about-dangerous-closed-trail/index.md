<p>What's OSM's policy here? Delete it? Or can I somehow mark it as dangerous/closed/difficult somehow?</p>
<p><strong>UPDATE</strong></p>
<p>Since people wanted more information about that trail, here's what I can say/found out:</p>
<ul>
<li>the trail was not mentioned anywhere on the official maps posted in the park</li>
<li>it is <a href="https://www.alltrails.com/trail/canada/british-columbia/rabbity-trail-mt-work-to-caleb-pike">marked "closed" in AllTrails.com</a></li>
<li>I did not see any signs that it was closed because we got onto it over a connecting trail and never saw the start/end of it</li>
<li>the trail was clearly unmaintained since the path was overgrown and very difficult to find</li>
</ul>

<div class="answer-body">
<p>Sounds like the same issue with "social trails" in some of the parks and forests in my area. The land managers don't want people on them for a variety of reasons (safety, resource restoration, etc.) but they are clearly visible in aerial imagery or on the ground.</p>
<p>My approach is to map them and then put an a access=no tag on them. Probably wouldn't hurt to put a comment or note tag with a human readable explanation.</p>
<p>I figure that covers the OSM desire to map what exists and yet give the land managers their due.</p>
<p>I don't tag things much with sac scale information as I am not as familiar with that as I should be, but if it is actually a dangerous trail there is probably a sac scale value that shows the difficulties that could be faced trying to follow it.</p>

<div class="answer-body">
<p>If the "danger" is just that one may have issues to find the path, then possibly <code>sac_scale=mountain_hiking</code> or <code>sac_scale=demanding_mountain_hiking</code> is the right tag for <a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Key:sac%20scale"><code>sac_scale</code></a>. Plus <code>trail_visibility=bad</code> for the <a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Key:trail%20visibility"><code>trail_visibility</code></a>. <a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Key:mtb:scale"><code>mtb:scale</code></a> may be a good addition.</p>
<p>For the parts which really have no path a good option would be to actually map no path there. This will stop most router using the path, but still show the real facts to experienced people who do manual route planning.</p>
<p>If you do the tagging this way, the only thing left is that the map/router (in your case the app komoot) actually uses this additional data. If it does not, please ask at the maintainer of the map/router.</p>
<p>Tagging it with some access tag (e.g. access=no) should require really a legal restriction to enter this path: that may be explicitly (by "do not enter" signs) or implicitly (because it is not marked as official route and because local/national law forbids walking on such routes).</p>
<p>If the path is that much unused that it is not longer a path tagging it with <a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Key:abandoned:"><code>abandoned:highway=path</code></a> instead of <code>highway=path</code> is the way to go. Afterwards it likely will not be shown anymore on most maps and not being used by routers like komoot.</p>
</div>
