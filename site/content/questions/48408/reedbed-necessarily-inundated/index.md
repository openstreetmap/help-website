<p>Hi Penegal, Use natural=wetland, wetland=reedbedas stated in the Wiki. A reed_bed could be or is in a fluctuating water level. If it’s growing in a polder, the water level will be in a constant movement up and down. A river could cause fluctuating water levels as well. Have a look here <a href="https://www.openstreetmap.org/#map=17/52.48704/4.7763">https://www.openstreetmap.org/#map=17/52.48704/4.7763</a> Reed is a plant that conserves the soil and makes it dry in time. The area has been designated as natural wetland and nature reserve, partly meadow and sometimes even very wet or flooded, boots necessary with reedbeds all around, the only way to make a survey complete is by boat. Its even named Waterland the surface and water levels will change permanently or seasonal.</p>

<div class="answer-body">
<p>Basically it should not be necessary to worry about apparent level of inundation. If the dominant plant is <em>Phragmites</em> (usually <em>P australis</em>) then this is by definition a reedbed.</p>
<p><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phragmites"><em>Phragmites</em></a> has specific requirements for mmoisture (its <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indicator_value">Ellenberg indicator</a> for this property is <a href="http://www.ecoflora.co.uk/search_ecochars.php?plant_no=1931090010%20&amp;cs=1">10</a>), so in practice we can use the presence of the plant to infer the regular presence of water. As <em>Phragmites</em> is rhizotomous a patch of reeds can extend both landwards and into lakes: usually the build-up of litter in drier parts of a reedbed lead to a succession as small trees and shrubs (notably willows &amp; alders) gain a footing.</p>
<p>One caveat, in some languages the vernacular term reed is applied to other plants: usually other grasses, sedges or rushes. In these cases avoid use of wetland=reedbed, and just retain the generic natural=wetland tag. Tagging for areas dominated by Sedges or <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cladium"><em>Cladium</em></a> is not well defined (although I hope to remedy the latter when I visit <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wicken_Fen">Wicken Fen</a> in the Autumn).</p>
