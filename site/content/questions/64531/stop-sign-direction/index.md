<p>Yes, the grey triangles indicate the direction the sign is facing. So in your picture, both signs appear to be facing the wrong direction, stopping traffic that has just turned onto the small street (alley?) rather than traffic turning from the small street onto Eugenio or Antonio.</p>
<p>You can modify the value of the <code>direction</code> tag and see its effect on the grey triangle. I assume that currently one of the signs is <code>direction=forward</code> and one is <code>direction=backward</code>, and if so, just reverse them.</p>

<div class="comment-text">
<p>Glad to help... Just to add -- it's very common to omit the <code>direction=*</code> on <code>highway=stop</code> nodes. The direction tag is never needed on one-way streets, and it's not needed at a crossroads unless traffic from at least one direction is allowed to pass through without stopping.</p>
<p>In the picture you posted, it's good to tag the direction but it may not be 100% necessary. To quote the wiki ( <a href="https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Tag:highway%3Dstop">https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Tag:highway%3Dstop</a> ), "direction can usually be deduced by finding the shorter distance to the priority intersection." The wording is slightly strange, but what it means is that a routing engine can guess the likely direction of a stop sign based on how close it is to the next road. But in my opinion it's better to be explicit in these cases, and your example is a good place to tag the direction of the stop signs to eliminate the guesswork.</p>
</div>

<p>You can add a direction=* tag to your stop sign nodes. The "direction" is either forward or backward depending on the direction the way is drawn in OSM. I don't see any arrowheads in your illustration to indicate the direction of drawing but if the way is running from south to north, then both signs would be tagged with direction=backward along with highway=stop. Otherwise, they would get a direction=backward tag.</p>
